# Workflow: Research & Report

## Purpose
When given any topic, conduct thorough research, organize findings, and produce a clean structured report saved to output/. Always clarify before researching.

---

## Phase 1: Clarify Before Starting

Before doing any research, ask the user these questions. Do not skip this step.

**Scope**
- How broad or narrow should the coverage be? (e.g. overview vs. deep dive on one aspect)
- Are there subtopics to include or explicitly exclude?
- Is this evergreen content or tied to something current/trending?

**Audience**
- Who is this for? (e.g. general public, athletes, clinicians, coaches)
- What do they already know? (beginner / intermediate / advanced)
- What do they want to walk away being able to do or understand?

**Angle & Intent**
- What specific question should the report answer?
- Is the goal to inform, persuade, compare options, or summarize evidence?
- Is there a particular bias or lens to apply (e.g. practical application over theory)?

**Output Preferences**
- Preferred length: brief (500 words), standard (1000–1500 words), or deep (2000+)?
- Should the report include a plain-language summary at the top?
- Any sections to definitely include or avoid?

Wait for answers before proceeding to Phase 2.

---

## Phase 2: Research

### Search Strategy
- Run at least 4–6 distinct searches using varied keywords and angles
- Search for: foundational overviews, recent studies or updates, expert opinions, practical applications, common misconceptions, and counterarguments
- Prioritize: peer-reviewed studies, reputable medical/sports science organizations, expert practitioners, established publications
- Avoid: low-authority blogs, unsourced claims, content older than 10 years unless it's foundational

### Source Requirements
- Minimum 5 credible sources
- At least 2 should be research-based (studies, systematic reviews, clinical guidelines)
- Note the publication date of each source
- Flag any conflicting information found across sources

### During Research — Track
For each source, note:
- Title, URL, publication date
- The key finding or claim it supports
- Credibility level (research / expert / practitioner / publication)

---

## Phase 3: Organize Findings

Before writing, sort all findings into these buckets:

1. **Core facts** — well-established, agreed upon across sources
2. **Nuance / caveats** — things that are true only under certain conditions
3. **Debate / conflict** — areas where sources disagree; note both sides
4. **Practical takeaways** — what someone can actually do with this information
5. **Gaps** — things the research didn't clearly answer

Discard anything that is redundant, weakly sourced, or off-topic given the user's stated angle.

---

## Phase 4: Write the Report

Save to: `output/<topic-slug>-report.md`
File naming: lowercase, hyphens, no spaces (e.g. `output/acl-recovery-report.md`)

### Report Structure

```
# [Topic Title]
**Date:** [Today's date]
**Audience:** [Who this is for]
**Question answered:** [The specific question this report addresses]

---

## Summary
[2–3 sentence plain-language overview of what this report covers and why it matters]

---

## Background
[1–2 short paragraphs: what is this topic, why does it matter, basic context]

## [Section 2 — tailored to topic]
[Findings organized by theme, not by source]

## [Section 3 — tailored to topic]

## [Section 4 — if needed]

## What's Still Unclear
[Honest note on gaps, conflicting evidence, or areas needing more research]

---

## Key Takeaways
- [Bullet: most important thing to remember]
- [Bullet: second most important]
- [Bullet: ...]
[3–6 bullets max — what the audience must walk away knowing]

## Recommended Next Steps
- [Concrete action the audience can take immediately]
- [Concrete action for the short term]
- [Concrete action for ongoing practice or follow-up]
[Practical, specific, and tied to the audience's level]

---

## Sources
1. [Title] — [URL] ([Date])
2. ...
```

### Writing Rules
- No jargon without a plain-language explanation immediately after
- Short sentences. Active voice.
- Bullets for lists of 3 or more items
- Bold key terms on first use
- Do not editorialize — stick to what the sources say, flag your own synthesis clearly
- If a claim is contested, say so explicitly

---

## Phase 5: Quality Check

Before saving, verify:
- [ ] Every claim links back to at least one cited source
- [ ] No undefined jargon
- [ ] Summary, Key Takeaways, and Recommended Next Steps are consistent with the body
- [ ] File saved to output/ with correct naming convention
- [ ] Sources section is complete with URLs and dates
- [ ] Report answers the specific question the user stated in Phase 1

---

## Triggering This Workflow

User says something like:
- "Research [topic] for me"
- "Write a report on [topic]"
- "I need to know about [topic]"

Always start with Phase 1. Never skip to research without clarifying first.
